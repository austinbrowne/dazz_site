## Hand Size

Hand size is the single most important factor in choosing a mouse. A mouse that's too large causes wrist strain; too small leads to cramping during long sessions.

**How to measure:** Flatten your hand on a ruler from the tip of your middle finger to the base of your palm.

- **Small hands (under 17cm):** You want mice under 120mm in length. Smaller shells let you maintain control without overextending your fingers.
- **Medium hands (17-19cm):** The sweet spot. Most mice on the market are designed for this range. You have the widest selection.
- **Large hands (19cm+):** Look for mice over 125mm in length with wider grips. Ergonomic shapes tend to work better than small symmetrical shells.

## Grip Style

Your grip style determines which shapes work for you. There's no "best" grip — it's about matching the mouse shape to how you naturally hold it.

- **Palm grip:** Your entire hand rests on the mouse. You want larger, ergonomic shapes with a pronounced hump that fills your palm.
- **Claw grip:** Your palm rests on the back of the mouse, fingers arched. Medium-sized mice with a rear hump work best.
- **Fingertip grip:** Only your fingertips touch the mouse. Small, lightweight mice with a low profile are ideal.

## Wired vs Wireless

This used to be a real debate. It isn't anymore.

- **Wireless:** Sub-1ms latency with modern 2.4 GHz dongles. No cable drag. Slightly heavier due to battery (5-15g). Costs $20-40 more. Needs occasional charging.
- **Wired:** Zero battery concerns. Usually lighter at the same price point. Better build quality per dollar since you're not paying for a wireless module. Cable drag is real, but paracords and bungees mitigate it.

**The verdict:** Go wireless unless you're optimizing every gram or every dollar. See the full [wireless mice guide](/guides/best-wireless-gaming-mice) for detailed rankings.

## Weight Preference

Mouse weight is personal, but trends are shifting toward lighter mice. Here's the general breakdown:

- **Ultralight (under 55g):** Maximum speed for flick-heavy games. Can feel insubstantial for some players.
- **Light (55-70g):** The current sweet spot. Fast enough for FPS, enough heft to feel controlled.
- **Medium (70-85g):** Provides more stopping power. Some players prefer this for slower, more deliberate aim styles.
- **Heavy (85g+):** Rare in modern gaming mice. Usually only found in older designs or mice with lots of features.

## Primary Game Genre

Different games reward different mouse characteristics. Here's a rough mapping:

- **Tactical FPS (Valorant, CS2):** Low weight, precise sensor, consistent shape. Flick aim and micro-adjustments matter most.
- **Battle royale (Fortnite, Apex):** Comfort for long sessions, wireless preferred, reliable tracking across large mouse movements.
- **MMO/MOBA (WoW, LoL):** Comfort over speed. Extra buttons are nice but not essential. Ergonomic shapes reduce fatigue.
- **General / mixed gaming:** A versatile, comfortable mouse that does everything well.

## FAQ

### Does sensor matter that much anymore?

Not really. Any mouse with a PAW3395, PAW3950, HERO 2, or Focus Pro sensor will track flawlessly for gaming. The days of meaningful sensor differences are over. Don't pay a premium for sensor specs alone.

### How important is polling rate?

1000 Hz is fine for the vast majority of players. 4000 Hz reduces latency by about 0.75ms — measurable but not game-changing. Don't make purchasing decisions based on polling rate.

### Should I buy the same mouse a pro player uses?

No. Pro players pick mice based on their hand size and grip, not because the mouse itself is magic. Find a mouse that fits YOUR hand. That said, if a pro with similar hand size and grip style likes a particular mouse, it's a reasonable data point.

### How do I know if a mouse is the right size?

If your fingertips reach the front of the mouse comfortably in your grip style, and the back of the mouse sits where you want it (filling your palm for palm grip, supporting the base of your palm for claw grip, or not touching your palm at all for fingertip grip), the size is right.

### Is it worth upgrading from a mouse that already works?

If your current mouse tracks well and feels comfortable, the honest answer is probably not. Marginal improvements in weight or wireless tech won't dramatically change your gameplay. Upgrade when something is genuinely wrong — discomfort, inconsistent tracking, or a broken scroll wheel.
